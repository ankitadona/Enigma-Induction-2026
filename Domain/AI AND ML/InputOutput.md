Flywheels represent one of the oldest and most elegant forms of mechanical energy storage, yet they are experiencing a major renaissance in modern engineering. At its core, a flywheel is a heavy revolving mass used to store rotational kinetic energy. By accelerating a rotor to very high speeds and maintaining the energy in the system as rotational momentum, flywheels effectively act as mechanical batteries. When energy is extracted from the system to generate electricity, the flywheel's rotational speed naturally decreases due to the conservation of energy. Conversely, feeding energy back into the system accelerates the rotor, "charging" the mechanical battery.
In recent years, the global transition toward renewable energy and the pressing need for grid stability have pushed flywheel technology into the spotlight. Unlike traditional chemical batteries, such as lithium-ion, flywheel energy storage systems (FESS) do not suffer from capacity degradation over thousands of charge cycles. They can operate efficiently across a wide range of extreme temperatures and offer incredibly rapid response times, making them ideal for frequency regulation and load leveling in modern power grids.
